RADAR Basic Theory:

Since the discovery of the existence of the electromagnetic wave, many applications have been developed to solve different types of problems. Radio Detection And Ranging have been one of the most popular of them, using waves at microwave frequencies. With it, the position and speed of an object can be detected even if it is not visible to the naked eye. With RADAR, objects can be detected from thousands of kilometers (depending on the medium and power levels), even through rain or fog. This technology was adapted very fast for military purposes during WWII, playing a key factor on the course of events. Its working principle is fairly simple: A microwave source generates a pulse that is emitted through an antenna, travels through the medium (commonly air), reflected (echo) from an object, and received back (Figure 1). From the echo time delay it is possible to compute the object’s distance from the source, and from the frequency shift, the speed of it can be also calculated. The ratio on between the transmitted power density St, and the scattered power Ps is called Radar Cross Section (RCS) or σ [1]. The main types of Radar are: monostatic and bistatic. In the Monostatic system, the transmit antenna is the same receive antenna. For the bistatic case, two different antennas are use, one to transmit and a second one to receive. Although the RCS value is function of the position of the antennas, the phenomenon and the basic relationships are the same.

Other important basic aspects of radar are the pulse repetition frequency (PRF) and the pulse width. The pulse with will determine the minimum resolution while detecting multiple objects. If the objects are close to each other and the pulse width is too long, then the echoes can overlap, giving the reading of one large object, rather than two small objects. Figure 2 illustrates this phenomenon [2]. On the other hand, the PRF determines the maximum radar range. For instance, for a given pulse, its echo is expected to be received before the next pulse is emitted. In the case that the first echo is received after the second pulse is transmitted, then it will not be possible to now if it corresponds to the first or the second echo. This is the reason why the PRF has to be selected accordingly with the expected maximum read range. The speed of the object can also be resolved by determining the frequency shift of the received echo, when compared with the transmitted pulse. This shift is generated by the Doppler effect.

Different techniques and methods are used to improve the system’s performance. For example, with pulse frequency modulation (FM) and a filter with a significant group delay at the receiver; the resolution can be improved by pulse compression. Nowadays, in the military field, Electronic Intelligence (ELINT) and Electronic Warfare (EW) technologies are being applied to RADAR systems.  For example, ELINT can be applied to analyze the RADAR signal being generated from an object, and detect the nature of the platform attached to it. If the object is generating a fast PRF, it might indicate that is a short range missile’s terminal scanning, while a slow PRF can be a sign of the presence of a long range system of a ship [2]. Moreover, many other applications are being developed in the area of human vital signs monitoring. It had been demonstrated that it is possible to use RADAR to detect respiratory and cardiac frequencies, as well as voice from detecting the movement of the throat.

Since its definition during the 1940’s, Radar is a technology that involves microwave circuits, antennas, and deep understanding of the electromagnetic waves behavior in presence of different mediums and objects. This technology will still be paramount for microwave engineer for a long time, with many applications being continuously developed.
